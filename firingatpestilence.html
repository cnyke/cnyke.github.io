Firing at Pestilence

June 20, 2022 | Claire Nyquist

Train your biggest gun on pestilence, and fire.

Nadia carefully developed the technique over years. In the little machines, the DNA was squeezed between the nanopores. The electric molecules ticked away at the strands of nucleic acids like a miniscule atomic metronome, while the interminable one hundred seconds to midnight of the doomsday clock twitched under stress. In the computer, data accumulated. A cryptic string of adenosines, guanines, thymines and cytosines ballooned from bytes into gigabytes. In the literature they called it community genomics, environmental genomics, ecogenomics, metagenomics. Sample from the environment and sequence the genetic material of everything you discover.

In the beginning, the data was overwhelming. Human DNA wildly engulfed the less prolific strands of the sample, the pathogens Nadia sought. But she developed muting methods for the abundance of human genetic material. It superseded the roulette of shotgun sequencing, where tiny and random strands were characterized. It supplanted the under-the-lamppost-because-that’s-where-the-light-is myopia of the polymerase chain reaction, which required an initial template.

Once small and reliable enough, the little machines were unfurled around the world. First, only the most advanced emergency departments in the wealthy world had one. Investment spawned innovation, and development spiraled on. Accuracy increased, while costs of the machines and their reagents decreased. Soon every other patient-physician interface had one too. They became as ubiquitous in labs around the world as microscopes were for blood smears. The unity of animals, humans and environment known as One Health spread. The little machines were adopted in veterinary practices too, so that every cow that keeled over had its florid microbiome comprehensively analyzed.

In remote Australia, a farmer fed his horses. One fell ill. She breathed noisily, wheezed, and the veterinarian arrived. The vet got up close with the animal and took a swab from her muzzle. The sample surrendered no information by PCR. Within a week, the vet, too, was wheezing. She reported to the emergency room, where the little metagenomic sequencing machine determined that the virus was a novel hendra virus strain. A predictable surprise.

The antimicrobial resistance crisis loomed for decades, as Staphylococcus aureus evolved methicillin resistance. Gradually tuberculosis morphed from a treatable disease with a stern course of antibiotics into a belligerent chronic condition and then into a death sentence. Here too, metagenomic sequencing could help. The behemoth data yielded patterns about the healthy microbiome, within the lumen of the intestine and the surface of the skin. As the bacteriome was sampled, the commensal flora and the friendliest of animalcules—the mutualists—became clear, and then became a treatment. They grew these bacteria in culture and filled spray bottles with the biota. They spritzed hospitals to compete with MRSA and the other nemeses of humanity.

Nadia sampled the environment outside the human body; she developed an intricate model of the flying fox microbiome. She discovered secrets on the age-old question: why are bats perpetually reservoirs of pestilence?

The machine detected an avian influenza strain, isolated from a chicken in Bangladesh. The basic reproduction number of the avian influenza strain was never known, never measured, since it was detected before the spillover. Supply chains weren’t disrupted. National borders were not closed. Rapid vaccine development wasn’t necessary. Disparities remained, but were not exacerbated. No one in long term care facilities was vulnerable. There weren’t food shortages. Toilet paper didn’t become a meme.

When catastrophe upon catastrophe was averted, the nanopores were put to other uses. Nadia sampled a droplet of pond water, revealing thousands upon thousands of new to science but billion year old organisms that had resisted culture for centuries.

The Earth swung round the sun, and Nadia refined the technology. A few advancements jerked the paradigm. She sampled the Northwest Eifuku volcano, of the Pacific Ocean’s Champagne vent. The site spewed pure liquid carbon dioxide, rich barium, calcium, and silicon. To everyone’s surprise, the sample processed by the technological descendent of metagenomic sequencing—sufficiently advanced so as to be scarcely distinguishable from magic—yielded a life form with a silicon-oxygen backbone in lieu of the carbon chains of all previously known life. Astrobiology cracked open with this n of 2. The discovery shattered the Drake equation, dissolved as it already was into probability densities.

To learn what Nadia knows, expand metagenomic sequencing.
